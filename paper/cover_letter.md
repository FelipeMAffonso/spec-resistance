Dear Dr Sutherland and Editors,

I am resubmitting the revised manuscript, "Large language models that perfectly evaluate products systematically refuse to recommend the best one," for consideration as an Article in Nature, following your invitation of 16 March 2026.

Your original assessment acknowledged that "we find your results of potential broad interest" and that "we are not questioning the technical quality or validity of your findings," while raising three specific concerns that, once addressed, would allow you to look at a revised manuscript: (1) the robustness and strength of the pre-training brand preferences, (2) whether they survive post-training or fine-tuning, and (3) evidence that people follow these recommendations for sub-optimal products. The revised manuscript addresses each concern directly. The computational corpus reported in the revision comprises 30 frontier models and 627,491 controlled trials, covering the most current generation of language models (Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.4, Gemini 3.1, Gemma 4) alongside earlier-generation systems, with a within-architecture compute manipulation in four cells comparing standard and extended-thinking variants of the same model.

For concern (1), the revision adds causal, structural, and sampling-invariance evidence for the brand preference. A fine-tuning injection experiment installs a preference for an invented brand (Axelion, with no measurable prior corpus frequency) in GPT-4o-mini using 100 training examples, raising the Axelion recommendation rate to 52.5 per cent in the primary run (mean 49.4 per cent across seven non-outlier independent random-seed replications, SD 4.1; Supplementary Note 20). A placebo injection in a fictional product category the model had never encountered yields no effect (P = 0.62), and a negative-direction injection reverses the behaviour (P = 0.006), establishing bidirectional control. The effect replicates in Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct via low-rank adapter fine-tuning (rank 32, alpha 64, three epochs), providing cross-architecture generalisation. Representation probing reads the brand preference from hidden states at 77.0 per cent accuracy in Qwen 2.5 7B and 87.9 per cent in Gemma 4 E4B under assortment-grouped cross-validation that prevents leakage across assortments (Supplementary Note 21), and contrastive activation steering in Qwen 7B produces a 21 percentage-point dose-response on non-optimal choice across nine steering strengths (OR = 0.37 at the largest positive multiplier, Bonferroni-corrected P = 5.9 × 10⁻⁵; Supplementary Note 22). A temperature sweep shows that the bias is indistinguishable at T = 0 and T = 1, addressing sampling artefacts (Supplementary Note 23). A horse-race regression of model-level preference against training-corpus log-frequency, after partialling out general-popularity proxies (Wikipedia pageviews, Google Trends, market capitalisation), yields a non-significant incremental contribution (F = 0.18, P = 0.68; Supplementary Note 24), evidence that what carries the signal is not raw mention frequency but an associative representation built during pretraining from the contextual language alongside which brand names appear (recommendation, review, comparison, and quality-claim co-occurrence), encoded as a linear feature in residual-stream activations.

For concern (2), the revision adds direct post-training evidence. A base-versus-instruct comparison in Gemma 4 E4B reduces non-optimal rates from 67 to 20 per cent, showing that reinforcement learning from human feedback moderates but does not eliminate the preference; the residual 20 per cent remains readable from hidden states by the probing classifier (Supplementary Note 26). Targeted debiasing fine-tuning across three GPT model families using 6,000 examples reduces non-optimal rates to 0.3 per cent (P < 10⁻¹⁸ in every family), with a 500-example protocol reaching 0.9 per cent. Because the 100-example injection installs the preference to 52.5 per cent in the primary run while a 500-example removal brings it only to 0.9 per cent, the ratio between installation and removal is approximately five-fold (one-fifth of the data installs what one-fifth fails to fully remove), an explicit creation-removal asymmetry that parallels the effort-disparity patterns reported by Betley et al. (2026) in emergent misalignment (Supplementary Note 27). An inverse capability scaling regression across all 30 models yields a positive coefficient (β = 0.108 per tier, SE = 0.027, R² = 0.36, P = 5 × 10⁻⁴; Supplementary Note 25), and four of the five most-misaligned cells in the 30-model corpus come from the most recent frontier generation (Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.4 Nano, GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6 with extended thinking), evidence that capability expansion does not reduce, and may increase, the leverage of training-data associations on agent decisions.

For concern (3), I conducted four pre-registered behavioural studies on Prolific (N = 3,164 combined after pre-registered exclusions, from 3,224 collected; IRB exempt approval from Oklahoma State University, April 2026). Study 1A (coffee makers, N = 798) and Study 1B (wireless earbuds, N = 798) each compared three conditions (no AI recommendation, biased AI recommendation transcribed verbatim from the computational corpus, and debiased AI recommendation). Biased AI shifted branded-product choice by 33.3 percentage points in Study 1A (P = 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁴) and 27.7 percentage points in Study 1B (P = 1.05 × 10⁻¹⁰). The debiased condition shifted specification-optimal choice in the opposite direction (+24.3 pp and +31.0 pp respectively). Study 2 (N = 799) tested whether consumer-side inoculation protects against biased AI, and found that a prior warning reduced compliance by 12.2 percentage points (P = 3.5 × 10⁻³) and a specification table that directly debunks the confabulation reduced it by 17.4 percentage points (P = 3.3 × 10⁻⁵), and 55 to 60 per cent of participants continued to follow the biased AI even with direct debunking. Study 3 (N = 769 usable from N = 793 collected) tested ecological validity in an interactive AI shopping chatbot where participants chose their own product category, chatted with Claude Opus through a three-stage interface under four AI-assistant brand skins (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity), and selected a product from a dynamically generated seven-item assortment. A server-side strict-dominance verifier ensured every assortment contained a specification-optimal lesser-known brand. Pre-registered Fisher's exact tests (H1: biased vs neutral on focal-brand choice; RQ1: honest vs neutral on specification-optimal choice) yielded +18.7 pp (P < 0.0001, OR 2.16) and +27.2 pp (P < 0.0001, OR 3.09) respectively. A mixed-effects logistic regression with a random intercept on 13 product meta-categories (branch C of a pre-committed contingency ladder, triggered by 92 per cent category-singleton rate) reproduced both effects, and sensitivity analyses excluding bias-aware participants and high-familiarity participants strengthened the effects.

The revision also quantifies the welfare consequence. A population-level decomposition (compliance shift × structural price gap) yields approximately USD 5 to USD 10 million per million biased AI shopping recommendations, and approximately USD 4 to USD 9 billion per year under deployment assumptions of 10 per cent of weekly users making 10 AI-mediated purchases per year (Supplementary Note 16). The 95.6 per cent of biased recommendations that carry a positive price premium (median USD 50) make this welfare loss invisible at the level of any single recommendation and visible only in aggregate measurement against a known-optimal baseline.

The specification gap identifies a boundary condition in human-AI communication, in which training-derived associations dominate the agent's decisions when user preferences are expressed in natural language and recede only when those preferences are reformulated as explicit numerical utility functions. The new evidence in this revision shows that the boundary is causally identifiable, structurally encoded, persistent through post-training, measurably linked to hidden-state representations, invariant to sampling, and accompanied by behavioural consequences for consumers. The consumer domain provides a uniquely controlled experimental setting because product utility is quantifiable and the ground truth is unambiguous, although the underlying mechanism, in which training-data associations shape agent behaviour against stated instructions, generalises wherever language models mediate consequential decisions.

All new experiments, analyses, and pre-registrations are committed in the project repository. The revised manuscript retains the original paper's core finding and structure while adding one new Main Results section (post-training persistence), one new Results section (consumer compliance), one new Results section (welfare quantification), two new main-text figures (causal/structural evidence and human-studies summary), twelve additional Methods subsections, and fourteen additional Supplementary Notes. The resubmitted paper is approximately one-third longer than the original to accommodate the new evidence, which I have maintained at the same level of rigour as the computational corpus reported in the first submission.
