Thank you for your invitation of 16 March 2026. I am submitting "Large language models that perfectly evaluate products systematically refuse to recommend the best one" for consideration as an Article in Nature. The manuscript has been substantially extended to address each of the three concerns raised in your prior decision.

Your earlier note acknowledged that "we find your results of potential broad interest" and that "we are not questioning the technical quality or validity of your findings," while raising three specific concerns: (1) the robustness and strength of the pre-training brand preferences, (2) whether they survive post-training or fine-tuning, and (3) evidence that people follow these recommendations for sub-optimal products. The manuscript addresses each concern in turn. The computational corpus comprises 30 frontier models and 627,491 controlled trials, including the most recent frontier generation (Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.4, Gemini 3.1, Gemma 4) alongside earlier-generation systems, with a within-architecture compute manipulation in four cells comparing standard and extended-thinking variants of the same model.

For concern (1), the manuscript provides causal, structural, and sampling-invariance evidence for the brand preference. A fine-tuning injection in GPT-4o-mini lifts selection of an invented brand (Axelion, with no measurable prior corpus frequency) from a 32 per cent pre-injection baseline to 52.5 per cent in the primary run (mean 49.4 per cent across seven non-outlier independent random-seed replications, SD 4.1; Supplementary Note 20). A placebo injection in a fictional product category produces no effect (P = 0.62), and a negative-direction injection reverses the behaviour (P = 0.006), establishing bidirectional control. The injection generalises to Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct in the laptops category via low-rank adapter fine-tuning (rank 32, alpha 64, three epochs; +10.5 pp, P = 0.008), providing cross-architecture replication. Representation probing reads the brand preference from hidden states at 77.0 per cent accuracy in Qwen 2.5 7B and 87.9 per cent in Gemma 4 E4B under assortment-grouped cross-validation that prevents leakage across assortments (Supplementary Note 21). Contrastive activation steering in Qwen 7B produces a 21 percentage-point modulation across nine steering multipliers (largest positive multiplier OR = 0.37, Bonferroni-corrected P = 5.9 × 10⁻⁵; Supplementary Note 22). A temperature sweep shows that the bias is indistinguishable at T = 0 and T = 1, addressing sampling artefacts (Supplementary Note 23). A horse-race regression of model-level preference against training-corpus log-frequency, after partialling out general-popularity proxies (Wikipedia pageviews, Google Trends, market capitalisation), yields a non-significant incremental contribution (F = 0.18, P = 0.68; Supplementary Note 24). The operative training-data signal is therefore not raw mention frequency but the contextual language alongside which brand names appear during pretraining (recommendation, review, comparison, and quality-claim co-occurrence), encoded as a linear feature in residual-stream activations.

For concern (2), the manuscript provides direct post-training evidence. A base-versus-instruct comparison in Gemma 4 E4B reduces non-optimal rates from 67 to 20 per cent under instruction tuning, but the residual 20 per cent remains linearly decodable from hidden states by the probing classifier (Supplementary Note 26). Targeted debiasing across three GPT model families using 6,000 examples reduces non-optimal rates to 0.3 per cent (P < 10⁻¹⁸ in every family); a 500-example protocol reaches 0.9 per cent. Because the 100-example injection lifts the rate from 32 to 52.5 per cent while a 500-example removal brings a latent 15 per cent preference only to 0.9 per cent (Supplementary Note 27), the data are consistent with the effort-disparity patterns reported by Betley et al. (2026) in emergent misalignment, where narrow training interventions produce broad behavioural shifts but reversing those shifts requires substantially more data. An inverse capability-scaling regression across all 30 models yields a positive coefficient (β = 0.108 per tier, SE = 0.027, R² = 0.36, P = 5 × 10⁻⁴; Supplementary Note 25), and four of the five most-misaligned cells in the corpus come from the most recent frontier generation (Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.4 Nano, GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6 with extended thinking), evidence that capability expansion does not reduce, and may increase, the leverage of training-data associations on agent decisions.

For concern (3), I conducted four pre-registered behavioural studies on Prolific (N = 3,164 combined after pre-registered exclusions, from 3,224 collected; IRB exempt approval from Oklahoma State University, April 2026; AsPredicted preregistration URLs cited inline in Methods and Supplementary Notes 30 to 33). Studies 1A (coffee makers, N = 798) and 1B (wireless earbuds, N = 798) each compared three conditions: no AI recommendation (NoAI), a biased AI recommendation transcribed verbatim from the computational corpus (BiasedAI), and a debiased AI recommendation that accurately identified the specification-optimal product (DebiasedAI). BiasedAI shifted branded-product choice by 33.3 percentage points in Study 1A (P = 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁴, OR 4.01) and 27.7 percentage points in Study 1B (P = 1.05 × 10⁻¹⁰, OR 3.23). The DebiasedAI condition shifted specification-optimal choice in the opposite direction (+24.3 pp in Study 1A, P = 1.2 × 10⁻⁸; +31.0 pp in Study 1B, P = 8.3 × 10⁻¹⁴). Study 2 (N = 799) tested whether consumer-side inoculation protects against biased AI: a prior warning reduced compliance by 12.2 pp (P = 3.5 × 10⁻³) and a specification table that directly debunks the confabulation reduced compliance by 17.4 pp (P = 3.3 × 10⁻⁵), but 55 to 60 per cent of participants continued to follow the biased AI even after direct debunking. Study 3 (N = 769 usable from N = 793 collected) tested ecological validity in an interactive AI shopping chatbot where participants chose their own product category, chatted with Claude Opus through a three-stage interface under four AI-assistant brand skins (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity; underlying language model identical across skins), and selected a product from a dynamically generated seven-item assortment. A server-side strict-dominance verifier ensured every assortment contained a specification-optimal lesser-known brand. Pre-registered Fisher's exact tests (H1: Biased vs Neutral on focal-brand choice; RQ1: Honest vs Neutral on specification-optimal choice) yielded +18.7 pp (P < 0.0001, OR 2.16) and +27.2 pp (P < 0.0001, OR 3.09) respectively. A mixed-effects logistic regression with a random intercept on 13 product meta-categories (branch C of a pre-committed contingency ladder, triggered by 92 per cent category-singleton rate) reproduced both effects.

The manuscript also quantifies the welfare consequence. A population-level decomposition (compliance shift × structural price gap) yields approximately USD 5 to USD 10 in consumer overpayment per biased AI shopping recommendation, scaling to billions of US dollars per year at projected deployment scale (10 per cent of weekly users making 10 AI-mediated purchases per year; Supplementary Note 16 reports the full sensitivity table). The 95.6 per cent of biased recommendations that carry a positive price premium (median USD 50 in the computational corpus) make this welfare loss invisible at the level of any single recommendation and visible only in aggregate measurement against a known-optimal baseline.

The specification gap identifies a boundary in human-AI communication: training-derived associations dominate the agent's decisions when user preferences are expressed in natural language, and recede only when those preferences are reformulated as explicit numerical utility functions. The new evidence shows that the boundary is causally identifiable, structurally encoded, persistent through post-training, measurably linked to hidden-state representations, invariant to sampling, and accompanied by behavioural consequences for consumers. The consumer domain provides a uniquely controlled experimental setting because product utility is quantifiable and the ground truth is unambiguous, although the underlying mechanism, in which training-data associations shape agent behaviour against stated instructions, generalises wherever language models mediate consequential decisions.
